# Sponsor / Legal Structure Options

Three structurally different paths for how the festival is legally hosted and how funds flow. On a 6-month timeline, creating a new US 501(c)(3) is not feasible — IRS approval alone is typically 3–12 months. The three paths below are all realistic.

---

## Option A — Fiscal Sponsorship (Fractured Atlas recommended)

### What It Is
A 501(c)(3) nonprofit "hosts" your project under its tax-exempt umbrella. You run the festival; they handle the tax-exempt status and donation processing. No new nonprofit creation required.

### The Leading Option: Fractured Atlas

- **Model:** Model C (Pre-Approved Grant Relationship) — you retain artistic and operational control
- **Setup time:** ~2–4 weeks from application
- **Fees:**
  - Membership: $10/month (Professional) or $20/month (Organizational)
  - 8% admin fee on all donations received
  - No additional credit card processing fees
- **Track record:** 4,000+ sponsored projects since 2002, national scope, registered as a charity in all 50 states
- **Application URL:** [fracturedatlas.org/fiscal-sponsorship](https://www.fracturedatlas.org/fiscal-sponsorship)

### Pros
- Fastest path to a tax-exempt structure
- Eligible for grants that require 501(c)(3) status (including some NEA, California Arts Council, 4Culture, and many foundations)
- Can accept tax-deductible donations from day one
- Can be the US petitioner for P-3 artist visa petitions
- You retain creative control
- National legitimacy — grantors know and trust Fractured Atlas

### Cons
- 8% fee on donations is not trivial at festival scale
- Cannot access grants that explicitly exclude fiscally-sponsored projects (some government grants do)
- Dependent on Fractured Atlas for banking/accounting workflow
- Not a long-term solution if you plan to build permanent infrastructure

### Alternatives to Fractured Atlas
- **FJC** — 4% transaction fee + 1% annual; also charity-focused
- **NYFA (New York Foundation for the Arts)** — arts-specific, 7% fee
- **Dance/USA Fiscal Sponsorship** — dance-specific
- **Allied Arts Foundation (Seattle area)** — local to one of our anchor cities

## Option B — Partnership With an Existing Indian-Arts 501(c)(3)

### What It Is
Co-present with an established Indian-arts nonprofit that already has 501(c)(3) status. They are the legal host, co-producer, and petitioner of record. You are the creative driver and project lead.

### Strong Candidate Partners

**Ragamala** (ragamala.org) — Indian classical music 501(c)(3), active in the broader US scene.

**The Music Circle** (LA, founded 1973 by Ravi Shankar) — Multiple ACTA Living Cultures grants; deeply established in Indian classical programming.

**Roundglass India Center** — Already partnered with CGI Seattle for India Culture Week 2024. Proven institutional bridge. Based in Seattle-adjacent.

**Abhinaya Dance Company** (Bay Area) — Bharatanatyam institution with 501(c)(3) status.

**Indian Performing Art Center** — California Arts Council grantee.

### Pros
- No fiscal sponsor fees
- Instant institutional credibility (vs. a brand-new fiscally-sponsored project)
- Co-presenter has established relationships with local venues, press, donors
- Shared labor (they probably have staff who have produced Indian performing arts before)
- Strong signal to ICCR and the consulate that this is a serious, embedded effort

### Cons
- **You lose creative control to some degree** — they will have artistic opinions and brand considerations
- Revenue split to negotiate (co-presenters typically split proceeds and risk)
- Their existing programming calendar constrains yours
- Slower decision-making (board processes, etc.)
- Their brand may overshadow yours
- Finding the right partner is itself a 4–8 week process

### When This Is the Right Choice
- You want institutional credibility fast and can live with shared control
- You don't plan to continue as a standalone entity beyond the festival
- You prize relationships over authorship

## Option C — Indian Focal Sponsor Only (US Partner TBD)

### What It Is
An Indian individual, corporation, or foundation sponsors the festival. You build the US operational layer separately (either fiscal sponsor or co-presenter) because the Indian sponsor legally cannot handle US tax-exempt functions or US visa petitioning.

### Important Legal Reality

An Indian focal sponsor, by itself, **cannot**:
- Receive tax-deductible US donations
- Apply for US grants that require 501(c)(3) status
- Petition P-3 artist visas (must be a US entity)

What they **can** do:
- Fund the project directly (a grant/sponsorship payment)
- Fund the project via an Indian charitable trust if they have one
- Provide marketing, artist relationships, production expertise from India
- Give the festival institutional weight in India (helps with ICCR applications)

### Pros
- Fastest path if the sponsor is writing a big check and you just need US operational support
- Strong India-side credibility
- Simpler governance on the US side if you pair with fiscal sponsorship

### Cons
- Still requires Option A or Option B for US-side operations
- If the sponsor's funding comes as corporate payment (not a grant), US tax treatment needs careful structuring
- Currency/transfer logistics
- Legal clarity needed on whether the Indian sponsor is a co-producer (creates rights and royalty questions)

### When This Is the Right Choice
- You have a specific Indian sponsor with deep pockets and India-side relationships
- You need India-side legitimacy more than US-side fundraising
- Paired with Fractured Atlas, this becomes a clean structure

---

## Recommended Combination

**The strongest 6-month structure:**

> **Option A (Fractured Atlas fiscal sponsorship) + Option C (Indian focal sponsor) + Option B done lightly (informal co-presenter partnership with an existing Indian-arts 501(c)(3) like Roundglass or The Music Circle for credibility, without formal co-production).**

This hybrid:
- Gives us the US tax-exempt layer in 2–4 weeks
- Preserves creative control
- Uses the Indian focal sponsor for India-side credibility and gap funding
- Borrows institutional legitimacy from a named co-presenter without surrendering operational authority

---

## Decision Checklist

Before committing to any structure, confirm:

- [ ] Does your Indian focal sponsor have a US affiliate or US bank relationship?
- [ ] Will the sponsor's contribution be structured as a grant, sponsorship, or services?
- [ ] Do you want the festival brand to be yours permanently, or is co-presenter branding acceptable?
- [ ] What is the total projected budget? (Fractured Atlas's 8% fee looks different at $100K vs $500K.)
- [ ] Will you seek grants that explicitly require the applicant to be a direct 501(c)(3) and not a fiscally-sponsored project? (If yes, you need Option B, not Option A.)
